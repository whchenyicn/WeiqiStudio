Learning Weiqi online is convenient, but a good book can still be extremely helpful.

A book gives you structure.

It slows you down.

It explains ideas carefully.

It also helps you build a stronger foundation instead of jumping randomly between videos, apps, and online games.

But there is one challenge.

Many Weiqi books are not written for complete beginners.

Some assume you already understand basic tactics. Some use older language. Some focus on advanced strategy before beginners are ready.

This guide will help you choose the best Weiqi books for your current level, so you can learn in the right order without feeling overwhelmed.

---

## Quick Recommendation

If you are a complete beginner, start with this order:

| Stage | Recommended Book |
| --- | --- |
| Complete beginner | Learn to Play Go, Volume 1 |
| Early beginner | Learn to Play Go, Volumes 2–3 |
| Practice problems | Graded Go Problems for Beginners, Volume 1 |
| Broad beginner review | The Second Book of Go |

| Better fundamentals | Lessons in the Fundamentals of Go |
| Fun modern introduction | So You Want to Play Go? |

If you only buy one book, start with:

**Learn to Play Go, Volume 1 by Janice Kim and Jeong Soo-hyun.**

It is one of the clearest beginner introductions available in English.

## 1. Learn to Play Go, Volume 1

**Best for:** Complete beginners  
**Recommended level:** New player to early beginner

This is probably the best first Weiqi book for most English-speaking beginners.

It introduces the game step by step and does not assume you already know how to play.

That matters because many “beginner” books are actually written for people who already understand the basic rules.

A true beginner needs something different.

You need clear explanations of:

- The board and stones
- Liberties
- Capturing
- Atari
- Basic life and death
- Territory
- Scoring
- How to play a real game

Learn to Play Go, Volume 1 does this well.

It is friendly, structured, and not too intimidating.

### Why it is useful

This book works because it respects the beginner’s perspective.

It does not rush too quickly into advanced theory.

It helps you understand what is happening on the board before expecting you to play well.

### Possible downside

If you already know the rules and have played many games, Volume 1 may feel too basic.

In that case, you can move to later volumes or choose a practice-focused book.

### Best way to use it

Read one chapter, then play a small 9×9 game.

Do not read the entire book without playing.

Weiqi concepts become much clearer when you experience them on the board.

## 2. Learn to Play Go Series

**Best for:** A complete beginner learning progressively  
**Recommended level:** Beginner to stronger beginner

The full Learn to Play Go series is a natural continuation after Volume 1.

Instead of giving you one big overwhelming book, the series builds gradually.

That makes it useful for beginners who want a guided path.

The early volumes focus on basic understanding, while later volumes introduce deeper concepts such as strategy, shape, attack, defense, and the endgame.

### Why it is useful

The series gives beginners a clear learning route.

Many new players struggle because they do not know what to study next.

This series solves that problem.

You can simply continue through the books as your understanding grows.

### Possible downside

You do not need to buy the whole series immediately.

Start with Volume 1 first.

If you like the style, continue.

If not, switch to a different book or add problem books instead.

### Best way to use it

Use the series alongside actual games.

After each chapter, try to notice the idea in your next game.

## 3. Graded Go Problems for Beginners, Volume 1

**Best for:** Practicing tactics  
**Recommended level:** Beginner

Reading explanations is helpful.

But Weiqi improvement also requires practice.

That is where problem books are useful.

Graded Go Problems for Beginners, Volume 1 is a good starting point for learning how to read simple positions.

It helps you practice:

- Capturing
- Atari
- Connecting
- Cutting
- Basic life and death
- Simple tactical reading

This kind of practice is extremely valuable for beginners.

### Why it is useful

Many beginners lose games because they miss simple tactical moves.

They fail to notice atari.

They miscount liberties.

They think a group is safe when it is not.

Problem books help train your eyes to see these situations faster.

### Possible downside

Do not choose problems that are too hard.

If you are guessing every answer, the book is probably above your current level.

Start easy.

Accuracy is more important than difficulty.

### Best way to use it

Solve a few problems each day.

Do not rush.

Try to read the answer in your head before checking.

Even five minutes of problem practice can help.

## 4. The Second Book of Go

**Best for:** Beginners who know the rules but feel lost  
**Recommended level:** Beginner to double-digit kyu

The Second Book of Go is often recommended after you already understand the basic rules.

It covers many practical topics that beginners need after their first few games.

This is useful because many beginners reach a point where they can play, but they still feel confused.

They understand capturing.

They understand territory.

But they do not know what to study next.

The Second Book of Go helps fill that gap.

### Why it is useful

It gives you a broad overview of important beginner topics.

Instead of focusing on only one skill, it introduces several areas of the game.

This makes it a good bridge between learning the rules and studying more specialized books.

### Possible downside

It may be too much for someone who has never played before.

If you are a total beginner, start with Learn to Play Go first.

### Best way to use it

Read it slowly.

When a chapter feels difficult, play more games before continuing.

You do not need to understand everything immediately.

## 5. Opening Theory Made Easy

**Best for:** Learning simple opening principles  
**Recommended level:** Beginner with some game experience

Beginners often ask:

**Where should I play at the beginning of the game?**

Opening Theory Made Easy is useful because it teaches opening ideas through practical principles.

This is better than memorizing long sequences.

As a beginner, you do not need advanced joseki.

You need simple principles, such as:

- Corners are important.
- Sides come next.
- The center is harder to control.
- Stones should work together.
- Do not play too small too early.

### Why it is useful

It gives beginners a way to think about the opening without becoming overwhelmed.

Instead of copying professional moves, you begin understanding why certain areas matter.

### Possible downside

Do not read this before understanding the basics.

If you still struggle with liberties, capturing, and territory, focus there first.

### Best way to use it

After reading a principle, try to apply it in your next few games.

Do not memorize.

Understand.

---

## 6. Lessons in the Fundamentals of Go

**Best for:** Strengthening your basic thinking  
**Recommended level:** Strong beginner to intermediate

Lessons in the Fundamentals of Go is a classic book.

Many players love it because it teaches important habits and attitudes, not just rules.

However, it is not always the best first book for a complete beginner.

It is better after you have played enough games to recognize the problems it discusses.

### Why it is useful

This book helps you think more seriously about fundamentals.

It encourages better judgment, better shape, better direction, and better discipline.

For many players, it becomes more valuable after they already have some experience.

### Possible downside

Some parts may feel abstract if you are too new.

If you read it too early, you may understand the words but miss the meaning.

### Best way to use it

Read it after you have played enough games to feel your own weaknesses.

Then the lessons will make much more sense.

---

## 7. So You Want to Play Go?

**Best for:** A modern, friendly introduction  
**Recommended level:** Beginner

So You Want to Play Go? is another beginner-friendly option.

It is designed as a progressive learning series and can be a good alternative if you want something approachable and modern.

Some learners prefer this style because it feels less formal than older Go books.

### Why it is useful

It introduces the game in a friendly way and can help beginners enjoy the learning process.

That matters.

The best beginner book is not always the most advanced book.

It is the book you will actually read and use.

### Possible downside

Different learners prefer different teaching styles.

Some may prefer the structure of Learn to Play Go, while others may enjoy this series more.

### Best way to use it

Use it as a guided introduction, especially if you want a lighter and more approachable tone.

## What Should You Read First?

If you are a complete beginner, follow this path:

### Step 1: Learn the rules

Start with:

**Learn to Play Go, Volume 1**

This gives you the foundation.

### Step 2: Play small games

Play 9×9 games online or with a physical board.

Do not wait until you finish many books.

You need experience.

### Step 3: Add problems

Use:

**Graded Go Problems for Beginners, Volume 1**

This helps train your tactical reading.

### Step 4: Broaden your understanding

Read:

**The Second Book of Go**

This helps connect different parts of the game.

### Step 5: Study specific topics

Later, add books on:

- Opening
- Life and death
- Tesuji
- Endgame
- Direction of play

Do not rush to advanced books too early.

## Books vs Online Lessons

Books are useful, but they should not be your only learning method.

The best approach is to combine:

- Books
- Online games
- Game reviews
- Beginner problems
- Videos or lessons
- Playing against real people

Books explain ideas.

Games test your understanding.

Reviews show your mistakes.

Problems sharpen your reading.

Together, they create much better progress.

## Should Beginners Buy Many Books?

No.

Buying too many books can actually slow you down.

It feels productive, but it can become a form of avoidance.

Instead of studying ten books at once, choose one beginner book and one problem book.

That is enough.

A simple beginner setup might be:

- One rule-and-concept book
- One problem book
- One online platform for games

That combination will take you much further than collecting many books you never finish.

## Common Beginner Mistakes When Choosing Books

### Starting too advanced

Many famous Go books are excellent, but not for complete beginners.

Choose books that match your current level.

### Reading without playing

You cannot learn Weiqi by reading alone.

Play games while you study.

### Skipping problems

Problem solving trains your reading ability.

Do not only read explanations.

### Buying too many books

More books do not automatically mean faster improvement.

One good book used well is better than five books left unfinished.

### Expecting one book to make you strong

Books help, but improvement comes from applying what you learn.

Here is a simple path:

| Stage | Book Type | Goal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| New player | Introductory book | Learn the rules |
| Early beginner | Beginner series | Build understanding |
| Beginner | Problem book | Practice reading |

This order prevents you from studying advanced concepts before you are ready.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best first Weiqi book?

For most beginners, Learn to Play Go, Volume 1 is one of the best first choices.

It is clear, structured, and written for complete beginners.

### Should I buy a problem book?

Yes.

A beginner problem book helps you practice reading, capturing, and life-and-death skills.

### Can I learn Weiqi without books?

Yes.

You can learn through online lessons, apps, videos, and games.

However, books provide structure and can help you build a stronger foundation.

### Should I read advanced Go books early?

Usually no.

Advanced books are more useful after you understand the basics and have played enough games.

### How many books do beginners need?

Start with one beginner book and one problem book.

That is enough for the early stage.
