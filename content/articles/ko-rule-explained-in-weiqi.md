Most Weiqi rules are simple.

You place stones.

You count liberties.

You capture stones by removing all of their liberties.

But eventually, you will meet one rule that feels confusing at first:

**The ko rule.**

Ko is a special rule that prevents the same board position from repeating forever.

Without the ko rule, two players could keep capturing and recapturing the same stone endlessly.

The good news is that ko is not as scary as it looks.

Once you understand the basic idea, it becomes one of the most interesting parts of Weiqi.

---

## What Is Ko?

Ko is a situation where one player captures a stone, but the opponent cannot immediately recapture it on the very next move.

Why?

Because doing so would recreate the exact same board position as before.

The ko rule prevents this.

Instead, the opponent must first play somewhere else.

Only after that can they come back and recapture the ko, if it is still available.

In simple terms:

**You cannot immediately repeat the previous board position.**

## Why Does the Ko Rule Exist?

Imagine this situation:

Black captures one white stone.

Then White immediately captures one black stone back.

Then Black captures again.

Then White captures again.

If both players can keep doing this forever, the game never progresses.

The ko rule solves this problem.

It forces the player who wants to recapture to make a move somewhere else first.

This keeps the game moving forward.

---

## The Basic Ko Sequence

A simple ko usually works like this:

1. Black captures one white stone.
2. White wants to capture back immediately.
3. White is not allowed to do so because that would repeat the position.
4. White must play somewhere else first.
5. Black may choose to fill the ko and end the fight.
6. Or Black may answer White's move elsewhere.
7. If Black does not fill the ko, White may return and recapture.

This back-and-forth is called a **ko fight**.

---

## What Is a Ko Threat?

A ko threat is a move played somewhere else on the board that your opponent cannot easily ignore.

Since you cannot immediately recapture the ko, you need to make your opponent respond elsewhere.

For example, you might play a move that threatens to capture a group or take valuable territory.

Your opponent then has a choice:

- Respond to your threat.
- Ignore it and fill the ko.

If your opponent responds to your threat, you may return and recapture the ko.

This is why ko fights can become very strategic.

They are not only about one small local area.

They can involve the whole board.

---

## What Does It Mean to Fill the Ko?

Filling the ko means playing on the empty point so your opponent can no longer recapture.

This ends the ko fight.

Sometimes filling the ko is the safest choice.

Other times, answering a large ko threat elsewhere may be more important.

A big part of ko strategy is deciding:

**Is this ko worth fighting for?**

Beginners do not need to master this immediately.

For now, just understand that filling the ko usually ends the cycle.

---

## Ko Is About Timing

The ko rule teaches an important Weiqi lesson:

Timing matters.

You may be able to capture a stone eventually, but not immediately.

You may need to create a threat first.

You may need to decide whether another area of the board is more important.

This is what makes ko interesting.

It turns a simple capture into a whole-board decision.

---

## Is Ko Common in Beginner Games?

Yes, but beginners often do not notice it at first.

Many early games include small ko shapes, especially around captures near the edge or corner.

At first, you may accidentally try to recapture immediately.

That is normal.

Most beginner-friendly apps or online servers will simply prevent the illegal move.

Over time, you will start recognizing ko shapes naturally.

---

## Simple Ko vs Complicated Ko

Not all ko situations are equally difficult.

A simple ko may involve one stone and one local fight.

Advanced ko situations can affect life and death, territory, endgame, and large groups.

Beginners only need to understand the simple version first.

Do not worry about advanced ko theory yet.

Your goal is to recognize:

- A repeated capture shape
- Why immediate recapture is not allowed
- How to play a threat elsewhere
- When a ko has been filled

That is enough for now.

---

## Common Beginner Mistakes

### Trying to recapture immediately

This is the classic ko mistake.

If immediate recapture repeats the previous board position, it is not allowed.

You must play elsewhere first.

---

### Playing a tiny ko threat

A ko threat should matter.

If your move elsewhere is too small, your opponent may ignore it and simply fill the ko.

---

### Fighting every ko

Not every ko is worth fighting.

Sometimes the ko is small, and a move elsewhere is more valuable.

---

### Forgetting the whole board

Ko fights often connect different parts of the board.

Do not stare only at the local capture.

Look for important threats elsewhere.

---

## Quick Recap

Here are the key ideas:

- Ko prevents endless repetition.
- You cannot immediately recapture if it recreates the previous board position.
- You must play somewhere else first.
- A ko threat is a move your opponent does not want to ignore.
- Filling the ko usually ends the ko fight.
- Beginners only need to understand simple ko at first.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why can't I immediately recapture in ko?

Because immediate recapture would recreate the same board position and could lead to an endless loop.

The ko rule prevents that.

---

### Can I recapture later?

Yes.

You may recapture after playing elsewhere first, as long as your opponent does not fill the ko.

---

### What is a ko threat?

A ko threat is a move elsewhere on the board that pressures your opponent to respond.

If they respond, you may return to the ko.

---

### Should beginners worry about ko?

A little, but not too much.

You should understand the basic rule, but advanced ko strategy can wait until later.

---

### Is ko difficult?

The basic rule is simple.

The strategy can become deep, but beginners only need the foundation first.
