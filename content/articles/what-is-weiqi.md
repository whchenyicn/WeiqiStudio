Weiqi is one of the world's oldest strategy board games. Played by two people using black and white stones on a grid, the objective is simple: control more territory than your opponent.

At first glance, the rules look surprisingly easy. Players take turns placing one stone at a time. There are no dice, no cards, and no hidden information. Everything happens in plain sight.

Yet behind these simple rules lies one of the deepest strategy games ever created.

Every move changes the board forever. Every decision creates new opportunities while closing others. A single stone may seem insignificant, but together they form living groups, surround territory, and influence the entire game.

Whether you know it as Weiqi, Go, or Baduk, this guide will introduce the game, explain why it has fascinated players for more than 2,500 years, and show you where to begin your learning journey.

## Why Is It Called Weiqi, Go, and Baduk?

If you're new to the game, you may have noticed that it has several different names.

They all refer to exactly the same game.

- Weiqi (围棋) is the Chinese name.
- Go is the Japanese name most commonly used in English-speaking countries.
- Baduk (바둑) is the Korean name.

Because the game spread across East Asia over many centuries, each culture developed its own traditions while keeping the same core rules.

At Weiqi Studio, we primarily use Weiqi because it is the original Chinese name. However, if you see "Go" or "Baduk" elsewhere, don't worry—they all describe the same game.

## A Game That Has Been Played for Over 2,500 Years

Weiqi is believed to have originated in ancient China more than 2,500 years ago, making it one of the oldest board games still played today.

Throughout history, it has been enjoyed by emperors, scholars, generals, artists, and ordinary families alike. Unlike many games that faded over time, Weiqi continued to grow across generations.

Eventually, it spread to Japan and Korea, where professional competitions and teaching systems helped the game flourish even further.

Today, millions of people around the world play Weiqi. International tournaments attract players from every continent, and online platforms allow beginners to find opponents within seconds.

Despite its ancient origins, Weiqi remains as relevant today as ever.

Its timeless appeal comes from something that has never changed: the joy of solving problems, creating ideas, and constantly improving.

## How Do You Play Weiqi?

One of the biggest surprises for beginners is how simple the basic rules are.

A game starts with an empty board.

One player uses black stones, while the other uses white stones.

Black always plays first.

Players then take turns placing one stone at a time on the intersections of the board's grid.

Unlike chess, stones do not move after they are placed.

Once a stone touches the board, it stays there unless it is captured by the opponent.

That single rule changes everything.

Every move becomes a permanent investment. Instead of moving pieces around the board, players gradually build positions, connect groups, protect weaknesses, and expand their influence.

Over time, the empty board transforms into a complex landscape of competing territories.

## What Is the Goal of the Game?

Many beginners assume the goal is to capture as many stones as possible.

It isn't.

Capturing stones is important, but it is only a means to a larger objective.

The real goal is to control more territory than your opponent by the end of the game.

Territory refers to the empty intersections surrounded by your stones.

Imagine two neighboring countries drawing borders.

Neither side tries to eliminate every citizen of the other country. Instead, both try to secure valuable land while defending what they already own.

Weiqi works in a similar way.

Sometimes capturing stones helps you gain territory.

Sometimes defending your own groups is more important.

Sometimes giving up a few stones allows you to gain much more elsewhere.

Learning to recognize these trade-offs is one of the most enjoyable parts of the game.

## Why Does Weiqi Feel So Different?

Most board games encourage direct confrontation.

Chess often revolves around attacking your opponent's pieces.

Many card games focus on collecting stronger cards.

Weiqi takes a different approach.

Instead of trying to destroy everything your opponent builds, you learn to balance attack, defense, expansion, sacrifice, and timing.

Two strong players may each build large territories while constantly influencing one another's decisions.

Conflict certainly exists, but so does cooperation.

Your opponent's moves create opportunities just as often as they create problems.

This makes every game feel unique.

Even after thousands of years, no two games are exactly alike.

## Why Do People Call Weiqi "The Game of Life"?

You may have heard people describe Weiqi as the game of life.

Although it is just a board game, many players discover lessons that extend far beyond the board.

For example, beginners often focus on winning every local fight.

Experienced players understand that sometimes losing a small battle leads to winning the entire game.

Likewise, they learn that chasing every opportunity usually creates new weaknesses.

Instead, strong players think about balance.

They ask questions like:

- Which area matters most?
- What can I safely give up?
- Where will this move matter ten moves from now?
- Is this move helping the whole board or only one corner?

These questions appear surprisingly often in everyday life.

Good decisions are not always about immediate success.

Sometimes they require patience.

Sometimes they require sacrifice.

Sometimes they require stepping back to see the bigger picture.

Many experienced players find that Weiqi trains habits such as careful thinking, emotional control, adaptability, and long-term planning.

Of course, Weiqi is first and foremost a game.

But perhaps that is exactly why these lessons become so memorable—they are learned naturally through play rather than through lectures.

## Why Is Weiqi Difficult for Beginners?

One of the first things new players notice is that Weiqi can feel overwhelming.

Unlike many games, there are very few rules telling you where to play next.

After your opponent places the first stone, you already have hundreds of possible moves.

That freedom is exciting, but it can also feel intimidating.

Many beginners ask questions like:

- "Where should I play?"
- "Which move is correct?"
- "Why did I lose?"

The answer is that Weiqi is not about memorizing one perfect move.

It is about learning principles.

As you continue through this beginner course, you'll discover simple ideas that make the game much easier to understand.

You'll learn why stones need breathing space, how groups stay alive, when to attack, and how territory is formed.

Little by little, the board begins to make sense.

What first looked like chaos gradually becomes a beautiful conversation between two players.

## Why Is Weiqi Worth Learning?

Many people first discover Weiqi because they are looking for a new hobby. Some stay because they enjoy the competition. Others are drawn to its history or culture.

Over time, however, many players realize that Weiqi offers something much deeper: there is always something new to learn.

Unlike games that can eventually be "solved" or mastered through memorization, Weiqi rewards understanding. Every game presents new situations, new ideas, and new challenges. Even professional players continue studying throughout their careers.

That makes Weiqi a game you can enjoy for a lifetime.

Here are a few reasons why millions of people continue to play it.

### It develops strategic thinking

Every move has consequences.

Playing a stone in one corner may influence a battle on the opposite side of the board many moves later. Learning to think ahead becomes a natural part of the game.

### It encourages creativity

There is rarely only one correct move.

Two strong players may approach the same position in completely different ways and both succeed. Weiqi rewards original ideas as much as technical skill.

### It teaches patience

Beginners often want immediate results.

Experienced players understand that good positions are built gradually. A single move may not look impressive today, but it can become the foundation for success later in the game.

### It is easy to start but impossible to finish

The rules can be learned in an afternoon.

The journey of improvement can last a lifetime.

For many players, that is exactly what makes Weiqi so rewarding.

## Your Journey Starts with the Basics

One mistake beginners often make is trying to learn everything at once.

They watch professional games, memorize advanced openings, or read complicated strategy books before understanding the basic rules.

That usually leads to frustration.

The best way to learn Weiqi is one step at a time.

Think of it like learning a language. Before writing essays, you first learn the alphabet. Before playing music, you first learn the notes.

Weiqi is no different.

The lessons in this beginner course are designed to build naturally on one another.

You'll start by understanding the board and the stones. Then you'll learn liberties, capturing, atari, eyes, territory, scoring, and finally how to play your first complete game.

By the end, the game that once looked confusing will feel much more familiar.

## Common Beginner Mistakes

Everyone makes mistakes when learning Weiqi. In fact, making mistakes is one of the fastest ways to improve.

Here are a few of the most common ones.

### 1. Focusing only on capturing stones

Capturing stones feels exciting, so many beginners chase every possible capture.

However, winning the game is about controlling territory—not collecting the most captures.

Sometimes the strongest move is to ignore a fight and build a larger area elsewhere.

### 2. Looking at only one part of the board

New players often become absorbed in a single battle.

Meanwhile, the rest of the board remains completely open.

A good habit is to pause before every move and look at the entire board. Ask yourself:

"Where is the biggest opportunity?"

### 3. Playing too close together

Many beginners place stones right next to their existing stones because it feels safer.

In reality, spreading out efficiently usually creates stronger positions and larger potential territory.

Learning the right balance between connection and expansion is one of the first strategic skills you'll develop.

### 4. Thinking every move has to be perfect

One of the biggest misconceptions is that strong players always know the best move.

They don't.

Even professionals review their games and discover mistakes.

Improvement comes from learning after each game, not from playing perfectly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is Weiqi difficult to learn?

The basic rules are surprisingly simple and can be learned in less than an hour.

What takes time is developing your understanding of strategy, which is part of what makes the game so enjoyable.

### How long does a game take?

It depends on the board size.

A beginner game on a 9×9 board may take around 15–30 minutes.

A full game on the standard 19×19 board often lasts between one and two hours.

### Should beginners start on a 19×19 board?

Most teachers recommend starting with a smaller board, such as 9×9.

A smaller board lets you experience capturing, territory, and life-and-death situations much more quickly.

Once you're comfortable with the basics, moving to 13×13 and eventually 19×19 feels much more natural.

### Do I need to memorize openings?

No.

Understanding the basic principles of the game is far more important than memorizing sequences of moves.

As your skills improve, opening patterns will become much easier to understand.

### Am I too old to learn Weiqi?

Not at all.

People begin learning Weiqi at every age. Some start as children, while others discover it after retirement.

The game rewards curiosity and consistent practice much more than age.
